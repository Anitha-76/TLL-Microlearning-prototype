Activity 2·15 min
How Learning Works
Here are four concepts that will shape how you think about your T127 project work and how you talk about it with faculty and the TLL team.
Learning Theories
One of the first things you will notice in T127 is that learning designers do not just pick activities they like. Every design decision is grounded in theory. A learning theory is a lens that helps us understand why people learn. Three theories have shaped instructional design since the 1950s, and you will encounter all three during your time with the TLL.
Behaviorism
Learning through response
Learning happens when the right behavior is reinforced. Practice, feedback, and repetition drive it. Think quizzes with immediate feedback, drills, and mastery checkpoints.
Cognitivism
Learning through mental structure
Learning happens when new information connects to what the learner already knows. Think analogies, advance organizers, concept maps, and structured explanations.
Constructivism
Learning through doing
Learning happens when people actively build meaning through experience. Think case studies, open-ended problems, reflection, and real-world projects. T127 itself is a constructivist experience.
The key insight for you as a designer
No single theory is always the right answer. The question we want you to start asking is: what kind of learning is actually needed here? If someone needs to memorize a process, behaviorism helps. If they need to understand a concept, cognitivism helps. If they need to apply judgment in a messy, real situation, constructivism helps. In T127, you will draw on all three.

Novice to Expert
Novice-to-expert progression is a core design tool. It describes how learners develop expertise over time, moving through recognizable stages, each with different needs, different ways of organizing knowledge, and different responses to instruction. As a learning designer, one of the first questions you should ask about any learner is: where are they on this spectrum? Because the answer will change everything about what you design for them.

What it tells you
Novices store knowledge as isolated facts. Experts store it as interconnected patterns. A novice approaching a new problem searches slowly for a rule or formula. An expert immediately sees structure, context, and connections. They are not just further along the same path. They actually think differently.
What you do with it
Design for where the learner actually is, not where you wish they were. Novices need clear structure, worked examples, and explicit connections. Open-ended exploration and ambiguity are things experts thrive in. For novices, that same ambiguity overwhelms and slows learning down.
The faculty challenge: the curse of expertise
Faculty are domain experts. That is why they are faculty. But expertise comes with a hidden cost: the longer you know something deeply, the harder it becomes to remember what it felt like to not know it. We call this the curse of expertise. It is one of the most common sources of misaligned course design in higher education. Faculty often design courses for learners who think like they do, not for the novices who are actually sitting in front of them. As a learning designer, your job is to be the voice of those novices in the room. You are there to ask the questions a student would ask: what does someone actually need to understand this, given where they are starting from?

Cognitive Load
Here is something useful to know about how the brain works. Your working memory, the part that handles new information in the moment, has a limited capacity. When a learning experience asks too much of it at once, the brain simply cannot process everything effectively. Cognitive load theory is the framework designers use to work with this limitation rather than against it.
🧩
Intrinsic
The inherent complexity of the topic itself. You cannot eliminate it, but you can sequence it wisely so learners build up to it.
🚫
Extraneous
Unnecessary complexity added by poor design. Confusing instructions, cluttered slides, irrelevant information. This is the load you want to eliminate.
✅
Germane
The productive mental effort of connecting new knowledge to what a learner already knows. This is the kind of load you want to encourage.
What this means for your design work
Good instructional design reduces extraneous load and sequences intrinsic load carefully so that learners have enough mental space left for the germane work of actually learning. So when a student says something feels overwhelming, that is often a design problem, not a student problem. That reframe is one of the most important shifts you will make in T127.

Design Models
If learning theories explain why people learn, design models describe how designers actually work. Think of them as thinking frameworks, not rigid checklists. Two models appear constantly in T127: ADDIE and Backwards Design. You will use both.
Design Model
ADDIE
Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, Evaluate. This is the process framework most TLL projects follow. It is not a strict sequence though. Evaluation feeds back into every phase, not just at the end.
Design Model
Backwards Design
Start with the outcome. What should the learner be able to do? Then design the assessment. Then design the activities. Most designers get this backwards and start with content rather than outcomes.
Bringing theory and model together
The model tells you what steps to take. The theory tells you why those steps will work. In your T127 project analysis document, you will need to show both: the design decisions you made and the learning science behind them. That connection between theory and practice is at the heart of what we do at the TLL.
